What the Law Covers

The law primarily targets semi-automatic centerfire rifles that have at least two of the following features: a folding or telescoping stock, a pistol grip that protrudes below the action, a thumbhole stock, a second handgrip, a grenade or flare launcher, a flash suppressor or muzzle brake, a threaded barrel, a bayonet mount, or a bipod.

Semi-automatic centerfire pistols with two or more qualifying features are also covered, as are semi-automatic shotguns with two or more qualifying features.

Not covered: Manually operated firearms (bolt-action, pump-action, lever-action, single-shot), antique firearms, and permanently inoperable weapons. If your gun requires a manual action to chamber each round, it is almost certainly not covered.

One feature alone is not enough. Your semi-automatic centerfire rifle needs at least two of the listed features before it qualifies as a covered firearm under this law. A pistol grip counts as one feature. If that's the only qualifying feature, the law doesn't apply to that rifle.

The most common combinations that tip a rifle over the threshold: pistol grip + folding/telescoping stock, or pistol grip + flash suppressor or threaded barrel.

Grandfathering — If You Already Own One

Permitted No. If you lawfully owned the firearm before July 1, 2026, the law grandfathers your ownership. You do not have to surrender it, register it, or modify it. The grandfathering clause protects continued possession and home use.

What the grandfathering does not protect: buying new ones, and most private transfers to other Virginia residents (see the Transfers section below).

Permitted Yes. The law explicitly exempts persons "actually engaged in lawful hunting or lawful recreational shooting activities at an established shooting range or shooting contest." Home storage and use on private property with the owner's permission are also permitted.

Permitted Yes, if it's secured. Virginia Code § 18.2-308(C)(8) exempts a firearm "secured in a container or compartment" in a personal vehicle. This covers general vehicle transport — you don't have to be headed to a specific destination. A locked case in the trunk or a locked container in the truck bed qualifies.

Walking with the firearm on a public street or sidewalk (not connected to a range or hunting trip) is a different question — that area of the law is still unsettled by the courts.

Transfers, Sales & Gifts

Permitted Yes — as a gift, with no money involved. The law carves out "the transfer of an assault weapon as a gift to an immediate family member" if you lawfully owned the firearm before July 1, 2026 and the recipient is not prohibited from owning firearms.

Immediate family is defined as: a spouse, children, parents, grandparents, and siblings. Friends, cousins, aunts, uncles, and in-laws do not qualify.

The recipient must be at least 18 and not otherwise prohibited. No background check is required for a gift (background checks cover sales, not gifts).

Prohibited Not for money. The law specifically uses the word "gift." A paid sale — even to an immediate family member — is prohibited. Virginia Code § 18.2-308.2:5(E) separately prohibits selling a covered firearm "for money, goods, services, or anything else of value."

If you want to benefit your brother, a no-cost gift is the legal path. Charging him anything — even $1 — makes it a prohibited sale.

Permitted Yes. Selling or consigning to a federally licensed firearms dealer (FFL) is an explicit exception in the law. This is one of the cleanest ways to legally part with a grandfathered firearm. The dealer handles all subsequent compliance obligations.

Prohibited No. Private sales of covered firearms to other Virginia residents are prohibited, even if both parties are otherwise law-abiding. Your options for getting rid of the firearm legally: sell to a licensed dealer, sell to an out-of-state buyer (through an FFL in their state), gift to an immediate family member, or surrender to law enforcement.

Conditional Yes, but not directly. You can sell to an out-of-state buyer who can lawfully possess the firearm in their home state — but federal law requires the transfer to go through a licensed dealer (FFL) in the buyer's state. You cannot hand it to them directly, even if they're standing in Virginia.

Age Rules

There are two separate age rules under Virginia's 2026 laws:

Purchase: You must be 21 or older to purchase a covered firearm (HB1525). This applies to buying from a dealer — there is no grandfathering exception for new purchases.

Possession: Persons under 18 are generally prohibited from possessing covered firearms (narrow exceptions for military, law enforcement, and JROTC/cadet activities).

Ages 18-20: Cannot buy a new one, but can continue to possess a firearm they lawfully owned before July 1, 2026. They can also receive one as a gift from an immediate family member.

High-Capacity Magazines

Virginia's 2026 law (HB1524) restricts magazines that hold more than 10 rounds when possessed "in connection with" a covered firearm. Key points:

Grandfathered: Magazines you lawfully owned before July 1, 2026 are protected — you can keep them.

Selling or transferring: Selling, gifting, or otherwise transferring a grandfathered magazine to another Virginia resident is generally prohibited, with limited exceptions (licensed dealers, law enforcement).

The "in connection with" limit: The restriction applies when the magazine is possessed alongside a covered firearm. A collector who keeps a high-capacity magazine separately from any covered firearm may be in a different situation — but this area has not been fully resolved by courts.

Penalties & Enforcement

Violations of the transfer prohibition (HB217/SB749) are a Class 1 misdemeanor — the most serious misdemeanor level in Virginia, carrying up to 12 months in jail and/or a $2,500 fine.

A misdemeanor conviction also triggers a 3-year disability from purchasing firearms under federal law (18 U.S.C. § 922(n)).

Violations of the public-carry ban (HB1524) are separately punishable.

Two circuit courts have issued injunctions blocking enforcement, but those injunctions are narrowly limited — they only bind the specific parties named in each case.

Lancaster County (Crump v. Katz): VSP cannot enforce here through December 31, 2026. Local prosecutors not named in the case may still act.

Washington County (Santolla v. Katz): Both VSP and several named Commonwealth's Attorneys are blocked through July 1, 2027. Despite some news reports, this is not a statewide injunction.

In the other ~127 jurisdictions, the law is in effect and can be enforced. The Virginia Supreme Court declined to consolidate the cases (July 6, 2026). The U.S. Supreme Court granted cert in related cases (June 30, 2026) with a decision expected spring 2027 — but until the Court rules, the law stands in most of Virginia.
